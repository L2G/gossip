The different scripts

All of the scripts do two things: they gather a scandal and its details from somewhere, then they send that information to one or more cronies. Decoding the cutesy metaphor:

  • A scandal is a short piece of text, like the Subject line of an email message.

  • The details are, usually, a longer piece of text, like the body of an email message. The distinction is important because some cronies, like Twitter, only get the scandal and not the details.

  • Cronies represent a destination for the information. Cronies can be sites like Twitter or Campfire, or they can be programs like email or Jabber.

Here are the different scripts:

  • fanout sends the scandal and details to the desired cronies.

  • svntell is intended to be called from a Subversion post-commit hook. It sends out information about the most recent revision.

  • watchdog is given a command to run. It does so, then reports on the command's output and how long it took.

fanout

fanout gets the scandal from its command-line arguments and the details from standard input. In the following, what you type is shown in bold: 

	$ ruby fanout this is a scandal
	Here are some details.
	  this is a scandal
	  Here are some details.

(The output is indented because that's how the standard output crony prints.)

Often, you want to send only a single line. In that case, the way fanout waits for input is annoying. You can turn that off:

watchdog
